Experiment 4 - Infinite Worlds

Description

This experiment showcases my work in using a tileset to work in an isometric environment to create a pseudo-infinite and pseudo-random world that is deterministic with a user-editable key. Tileset Credit: 32 x 32 Pixel Isometric Tiles by scrabling
The first world displays my attempt at recreating a grassland with rocks.
The second world displays my attempt at recreating Antartica with icebergs and water.
The third world displays my attempt at creating a swamp/pond combination with water, rocks, and grass.

Technical

  • Infinity: I used the infinite extent of my worlds to represent a grassland with rocks, Antartica with icebergs and water, and a swamp/pond combination with water, rocks, and grass.
  • Variation in Space: As the audience browses my worlds, theyâ€™ll see different variations of rocks, grass, ice, and water tiles set up.
  • Variation in Key: Different worlds will have the previously mentioned tiles spawning in different areas.
  • Signs of Life: Clicking tiles causes logs, rocks, or plants (that grow after a delay) to spawn while the passage of time results in the grass or water moving back and forth. Plants also grow when clicking a tile after a brief delay in the third world.

    • Reflection

    Working with p5.js this time was difficult because I had to take time to understand how the draw functions and tiles were set up. I found it difficult to properly place the correct tile in the right spot because of the isometric set up, but once I understood this, I used noise functions to create a varied world. I ran into some problems with the draw function because it updated every 60 frames, but I changed drawTile() so that each image would look properly rendered and not glitching. The integration of 3 canvases was similar to Experiment 3, but I decided to only have one text box to change all 3 worlds because it would be simpler to look at.
